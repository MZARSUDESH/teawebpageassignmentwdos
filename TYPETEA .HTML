TEA NAME THE DESCRIPTION HEALTH BENEFITS IMAGES
White Tea For the discerning tea drinker, white tea, known in Sri Lanka as Silver Tips, is among the most rare and refined of teas. While much of the "White Tea" called tea around the world is simply a combination of a negligible sum of white tea and green or black teas, true white tea preserves a particular appearance of a silvery leaf, with a velvety feel and when brewed, creates a vivid, golden liquor. Everyone today, from cooks to medical experts, praises the taste of white tea and its supposed health advantages, which is why we have seen white tea items hit the shop shelves and find a spot on menus for drinks. A high number of antioxidants, as well as polyphenols, flavonoids, and tannins are found in white tea. Such dietary advantages have a beneficial impact on our wellness and fitness. It has been shown that white tea protects the body from many illnesses and reduces the risk of cancer and cardiovascular conditions.
Green tea Green tea from China, also grown in Japan, Vietnam, Taiwan and increasingly in India and Sri Lanka, is unfermented, retaining usually moderate, light and often astringent properties. Until drying and sifting, the leaf is picked, withered, heated and then rolled. Several separate methods represent distinctive practices in parts of China, Taiwan and Japan for making green tea. Sencha, made using a process unique to Japan, is the most common tea in Japan. It has been shown that green tea increases blood flow and reduces cholesterol. Green tea helped to avoid a number of heart-related complications, from elevated blood pressure to congestive heart failure, a 2013 study of several studies showed. In persons with diabetes, green tea tends to help keep blood sugar steady. Since catechins decrease blood pressure and cholesterol, they can help protect against the harm that a high-fat diet can do.
Black tea Black tea, the most common kind of tea from Sri Lanka, India, Kenya and other former British colonies, is completely fermented. To decrease moisture and make the leaf pliable, black tea is picked, shrivelled, then rolled, fermented and dried (or red) before sifting. Black tea as a source of antioxidants is a primary field of interest. Antioxidants aid in fighting free radicals. Free radicals in the body are reactive molecules that arise from both natural processes and stresses on the environment.The risk of skin, breast, lung, and prostate cancers may be minimized by black tea.
Oolong tea Oolong teas are slightly oxidized or fermented, ranging from light "Springtime Oolong Teas" that have a soft, aromatic and moderate sweetness to heavier dark Oolongs that are woody, often earthy with strong fruit and sometimes caramelised notes. Sometimes 3 leaves and a bud, withered, fermented, then heated, rolled and dried, the teas are picked. People who drink at least 10 ounces of oolong tea a week were shown to have lower chances of elevated overall levels of cholesterol, triglyceride, and LDL or 'poor' cholesterol.
